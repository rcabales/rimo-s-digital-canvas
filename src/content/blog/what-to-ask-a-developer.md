## What to Ask a Web Developer Before You Hire One (And What Their Answers Reveal)

---

You've decided your website needs work. Maybe it's slow, outdated, or just not converting the way it should. You've started reaching out to developers - and now you're staring at proposals, portfolios, and hourly rates that vary by a factor of ten.

How do you tell the difference between someone who will quietly take your money and someone who will actually solve your problem?

The answer isn't in their portfolio. It's in how they answer your questions.

Here are the questions worth asking - and more importantly, what a good answer looks like versus a red flag.

---

## 1. "Can you walk me through a project where something went wrong?"

### Why this question matters

Every developer will tell you about their wins. You want to know how they behave when things don't go to plan - because something always goes wrong.

### Green flag answer

They tell you a specific story. A launch that had to be delayed. A feature that had to be rebuilt. A client who changed direction halfway through. What matters is that they describe *what they did*, not just *what happened*. Good developers take ownership, communicate early, and adapt. They don't blame the client, the tech stack, or bad luck.

### Red flag answer

They can't think of anything. Or they give you a vague, rehearsed non-answer: *"Every project has challenges but we always deliver."* That's not humility - that's a warning sign. Either they haven't done enough work to have a war story, or they've learned nothing from the ones they have.

---

## 2. "What would you NOT build with WordPress?"

### Why this question matters

WordPress powers over 40% of the web. It's also badly misused - slapped onto projects that outgrow it within 18 months, or chosen simply because the developer knows it well. Asking this question tells you whether the person you're hiring has genuine technical judgment or just a default tool they use on everything.

### Green flag answer

They give you real examples. Complex real-time applications. High-frequency e-commerce platforms with custom inventory logic. Anything requiring significant custom application architecture rather than content management. They might also explain *when* WordPress *is* the right choice - and why - which shows they're not just dismissing it either.

### Red flag answer

They defend WordPress for every use case without qualification. Or they go the other direction and dismiss it entirely as unprofessional. Both extremes suggest someone who hasn't thought critically about the tool. A good developer chooses the right technology for the problem, not the technology they happen to know best.

---

## 3. "Who owns the website when the project is finished?"

### Why this question matters

This question sounds almost insultingly basic. Ask it anyway.

Some developers retain ownership of code, hosting accounts, or domain registrations - sometimes deliberately, sometimes out of habit. If you don't ask, you may finish paying for a website you don't technically own, running on hosting you can't access, built on a codebase you have no rights to modify.

### Green flag answer

You own everything. The code, the domain, the hosting account credentials, the design files. They're happy to put this in writing. They'll also walk you through how to access everything independently, because they want you to be able to manage it without them.

### Red flag answer

Any hesitation. Any talk of "proprietary systems" or "our platform" that isn't clearly explained. Any arrangement where you pay monthly to keep the site running without a clear breakdown of what that money covers and what happens if you stop paying.

---

## 4. "How will you handle the site after launch?"

### Why this question matters

Most websites need ongoing attention - security updates, plugin or dependency management, performance monitoring, content changes, bug fixes. What you're really asking here is: *do you have a plan for what happens when I need help six months from now?*

### Green flag answer

They have a clearly defined maintenance offering. They explain what it covers (and what it doesn't), how quickly they respond to issues, and how billing works for it. They're also honest about whether their services are required for maintenance or whether you can hand it off to someone else.

### Red flag answer

*"We'll be available if you need us."* This is not a plan. It's a polite way of saying there is no plan. Without a defined support structure, you're at the mercy of their schedule when something breaks - and something always breaks. Ambiguity here tends to mean either expensive ad-hoc emergency fixes or a ghost who stops responding after the invoice is paid.

---

## 5. "Can you explain your approach to website performance?"

### Why this question matters

A slow website loses visitors. Google penalises it in search rankings. And yet, slow websites are everywhere - because performance is invisible until it's a problem, and many developers treat it as an afterthought rather than a design constraint.

### Green flag answer

They talk about performance as something built in from the start, not bolted on at the end. They mention specific things they do by default: optimising images, minimising unnecessary scripts, choosing lightweight frameworks where appropriate, using caching, measuring Core Web Vitals. They can tell you, roughly, what performance score they typically aim for and how they measure it.

### Red flag answer

*"We can install a performance plugin."* A plugin can help, but it can't rescue a fundamentally bloated site - it just applies band-aids. If performance optimisation starts and ends with a plugin recommendation, the underlying work probably won't be fast either.

---

## 6. "What do you need from me for this project to succeed?"

### Why this question matters

This one flips the dynamic. Most clients interview developers expecting to be sold to. Asking what *they* need from *you* reveals a lot about how they run projects.

### Green flag answer

They have a clear list. Timely feedback within a defined window. Content and assets by a specific date. A single point of contact so decisions don't get stuck in committee. Access to existing hosting or accounts. They're asking because they've been on projects that stalled due to client delays, and they're trying to prevent that - which means they respect both their time and yours.

### Red flag answer

*"Oh, don't worry - we handle everything."* This sounds reassuring. It isn't. Even the best developer needs your input - your brand, your content, your business logic, your feedback. A developer who claims to need nothing from you either hasn't thought the project through, or is planning to fill the gaps with guesswork.

---

## 7. "What happens if I'm unhappy with the result?"

### Why this question matters

You hope this never comes up. Ask anyway, before you've signed anything.

### Green flag answer

They walk you through a revision process: what's included in the scope, how many rounds of changes are standard, and how they handle genuine disputes. They won't guarantee you'll love everything - that would be dishonest - but they'll describe a fair process for working through disagreements and a clear endpoint.

### Red flag answer

Irritation, defensiveness, or a pivot to talking about their track record. *"Our clients are always happy"* is not a dispute resolution policy. If a developer can't calmly explain what happens when a client isn't satisfied, that's the information you needed.

---

## One thing to pay attention to beyond the answers

Notice how quickly they get to asking about *you*.

A good developer is curious about your business - not just your budget and deadline. They want to understand what your site is actually supposed to do. Who visits it. What action you want those visitors to take. What's been tried before and why it didn't work.

If you spend 45 minutes on a call and the developer never once asks about your customers, you're being sold a website rather than having a problem solved. Those are different transactions, and only one of them tends to end well.

---

## The uncomfortable truth about price

The cheapest quote is almost never the cheapest option. Low-cost projects tend to mean one of three things: the scope has been quietly trimmed to fit the budget, the developer is inexperienced, or the work will be done fast and handed off with little care for what comes after.

None of those outcomes are actually cheap. They just defer the cost.

That said - a high price is not a guarantee of quality. Use these questions to evaluate the person, not just the number. A developer who answers thoughtfully, speaks plainly about limitations, and asks good questions about your business is worth more than one who gives polished non-answers and sends you a beautiful proposal deck.

You're not just buying a website. You're starting a working relationship. These questions help you understand who you're really dealing with before the contract is signed.
