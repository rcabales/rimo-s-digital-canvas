*Or: how to tell if your digital shopfront is quietly losing you business*

---

Here's a slightly uncomfortable truth: most business owners have no idea how their website comes across to strangers.

You've looked at it so many times that you've gone completely blind to it. You know where everything is, you know what you meant by that tagline, and you've long since stopped noticing that the photo on the homepage is a bit blurry.

Your visitors, however, are seeing it with fresh eyes. And first impressions on the web are *brutal* — research suggests people form an opinion about a website in about 0.05 seconds. That's faster than a sneeze.

So let's have an honest look at the warning signs. How many of these sound familiar?

---

## 1. It Takes Ages to Load

We have all become spectacularly impatient on the internet. If your website takes more than a couple of seconds to load, a significant chunk of your visitors will simply leave. Not because they're rude — just because there are a hundred other options a click away and life is short.

Slow websites are usually caused by oversized images, cheap hosting, or a template stuffed full of code you're not even using. It's the digital equivalent of a shop where the door sticks — most people will just move on to the next one.

**The tell:** Open your website on your phone using mobile data (not your home Wi-Fi). If you find yourself drumming your fingers, your customers are already gone.

---

## 2. It Looks Broken on Mobile

Here's a stat worth knowing: more than half of all web traffic now comes from mobile devices. If your website was designed primarily for desktop and sort of *hopes for the best* on a phone, you're turning away the majority of your visitors before they've even read a word.

Broken mobile layouts are easy to spot — text that runs off the edge of the screen, buttons too small to tap without zooming in, images stacked in strange ways, menus that don't open. It looks unfinished. And unfinished doesn't exactly inspire confidence in your professionalism.

**The tell:** Pull up your website on your phone right now. Be honest with yourself.

---

## 3. Nobody Can Figure Out What You Actually Do

This one is more common than you'd think. You're so close to your own business that it's obvious to you what you offer. But a visitor who's landed on your homepage for the first time should be able to understand what you do, who it's for, and how to get in touch — within about five seconds.

If your homepage leads with a vague tagline like *"Empowering Solutions for a Connected World"* and then a big stock photo of people shaking hands... that's not clarity, that's camouflage.

**The tell:** Ask someone who knows nothing about your business to look at your homepage for five seconds, then close it. Ask them what you do. If they can't tell you, you have a problem.

---

## 4. Your Contact Details Are Hard to Find

This one should be simple, and yet. If someone has made it through your website, liked what they've seen, and decided they want to get in touch — the very last thing you want is to make that difficult. And yet contact pages are buried, phone numbers are missing, and contact forms vanish into the void without so much as a confirmation message.

Every extra click between "I'm interested" and "I've made contact" is an opportunity for someone to change their mind, get distracted, or give up entirely.

**The tell:** Time yourself finding your own contact details from your homepage. If it takes more than two clicks, it's too many.

---

## 5. It Looks Like It Was Built in 2009

Design trends move on. What looked sharp and modern fifteen years ago can now read as neglected — and neglected websites suggest neglected businesses. We make these judgements automatically and often unfairly, but we make them nonetheless.

This doesn't mean you need to rebuild your site every two years. It means avoiding things like: tiny text, clashing colour schemes, stock photos of people in headsets, auto-playing music (yes, this still happens), and Flash animations (please, no).

A clean, modern design signals that you're active, current, and paying attention.

**The tell:** Look at websites of businesses you admire. Now look at yours. Is there a jarring difference?

---

## So What Now?

If you've read through this list with a growing sense of recognition, don't panic — these are all fixable problems. The first step is just knowing they exist.

A website that loads quickly, looks great on any device, clearly explains what you offer, and makes it easy to get in touch isn't a luxury. It's the baseline for not losing business you didn't even know you were losing.
